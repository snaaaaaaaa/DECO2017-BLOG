In this week’s tutorial, my teammate and I presented our various ideas to each other, weighing the opportunities, benefits, and feasibility of each idea. We considered my previously discussed communities of photographers and home cooks, as well as surfers, thrift shoppers and finding hidden food spots around Sydney. Ultimately, we decided on a community hub for home cooks, as we both had interest in the subject matter, and more practically, it is straightforward to source reliable recipe data to build the prototype with. 

With a community selected, we began to brainstorm possible pages and features the site could include. 

[FEATURES LIST]

However, before making concrete decisions, we decided on key methods of user research to aid our discovery phase, in order to derive informed insights which we can then base our design off of. Considering the time we had available and the scope of our project, we chose to conduct online ethnography, and competitor analysis, where I was responsible for the latter method. 

[COMPETITOR ANALYSIS]

The brief continually underlines the importance of personalisation to the community hub, and that this tailor-made experience is what sets BlaBla Corp apart from other community sites. Hence, we felt that it is important to conduct a competitor analysis of existing sites, noting and comparing their features to aspects of the design which were highlighted in the brief: site layout, community structure, advertising, features which encourage continued engagement, as well as personalisation. 

After consolidating our research, we then concluded the following design insights:

1. Due to the variety of users likely to use this community hub, accessibility, while always important, becomes especially paramount in this circumstance. It also aids cooks who are less confident in tech usage and navigating such community websites. 
2. The core, stand-out feature of this community hub needs to be unique to the experience of being a home cook, and should be a feature not offered on the analysed competitor sites. It should go beyond uploading a photo of a recipe with a caption, and should instead be a streamlined feature tailored to recipes, with clear tags and a set format. 
3. Time is an essential aspect to the experience of cooks, which must be prioritised when designing website features. The web app should be quick and convenient to navigate through recipes, potentially through a structured and detailed filtering system. A featured recipe section can also add to convenience, highlighting recipes deemed reliable by the community.

Moreover, the primary device for this web app will be desktop. Though many users use phones to access community apps, in the context of a community for home cooks which will have to show many different visual and textual elements at once, a desktop screen will allow for the most visual cohesion and clarity.
